This post explores how modern quant funds use machine learning for trading, from feature engineering to model deployment.

## The Quant ML Pipeline

### 1. Data Engineering

Raw market data is messy. The first step is cleaning and normalizing:

- **Price normalization**: Adjusting for splits, dividends, corporate actions
- **Time alignment**: Synchronizing data from multiple sources
- **Outlier detection**: Identifying and handling bad ticks

### 2. Feature Engineering

Good features matter more than complex models:

**Technical indicators**:
- Moving averages (simple, exponential)
- Momentum indicators (RSI, MACD)
- Volatility measures (Bollinger Bands, ATR)

**Microstructure features**:
- Order book imbalance
- Bid-ask spread dynamics
- Trade flow toxicity (VPIN)

**Alternative data**:
- Sentiment from news and social media
- Satellite imagery (parking lots, crop health)
- Credit card transaction aggregates

### 3. Model Architecture

Modern quant funds use a mix of models:

**Linear models** (still popular for their interpretability):
$$r_t = \beta_0 + \sum_{i} \beta_i f_{i,t} + \epsilon_t$$

**Tree-based models** (XGBoost, LightGBM):
- Handle non-linear relationships
- Robust to outliers
- Fast inference

**Neural networks**:
- **MLPs**: For tabular factor data
- **RNNs/LSTMs**: For time series patterns
- **Transformers**: For multi-asset attention mechanisms

### 4. Training Consideration

**Purging and embargo**: Prevent information leakage by removing overlapping periods

**Walk-forward analysis**: Train on past, validate on recent, test on future

**Regime detection**: Markets change. Models should adapt or be specialized for different regimes.

### 5. Risk Management

Machine learning models can find spurious correlations:

- **Position sizing**: Kelly criterion or mean-variance optimization
- **Stop losses**: Hard and trailing stops
- **Drawdown controls**: Reduce exposure during losing streaks

## From Prediction to Portfolio

A trading system needs more than return forecasts:

1. **Alpha model**: Predict expected returns
2. **Risk model**: Estimate covariance matrix
3. **Transaction cost model**: Estimate market impact
4. **Portfolio construction**: Optimize weights given constraints

The optimizer balances expected return against risk and costs:

$$\max_{w} \left( \alpha^T w - \lambda w^T \Sigma w - \text{TC}(w) \right)$$

Subject to:
- Budget constraint: $\sum_i w_i = 1$
- Position limits: $|w_i| \leq c_i$
- Sector exposure: $|w_{\text{sector}}| \leq s$

## Practical Challenges

### Overfitting

With thousands of features and ML models, overfitting is inevitable:

- Use simple models when possible
- Regularization (L1, L2, dropout)
- Ensemble methods
- Paper trading for extended periods

### Market Efficiency

If a signal is profitable, others will find it:

- Signals decay over time
- Need continuous research pipeline
- Combination of signals more stable than individual signals

### Execution

Predictions are worthless without execution:

- **Market impact**: Large orders move prices
- **Latency**: Speed matters for high-frequency signals
- **Fill rates**: Not all intended trades execute

## Conclusion

Quantitative trading with ML is hard. The easy alpha has been arbitraged away. Success requires:

1. Novel data sources
2. Sophisticated feature engineering
3. Robust model validation
4. World-class execution infrastructure

The firms that combine these elements will continue to generate alpha. The rest will become case studies in the difficulty of trading.
