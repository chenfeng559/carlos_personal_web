## The Gap Between Logic and Learning

Classical logic is symbolic: propositions are discrete, rules are exact, and deduction is certain. Neural networks are sub-symbolic: representations are distributed, inference is approximate, and learning is statistical.

Can we bridge this gap?

## Tensor Logic

Tensor logic treats logical operations as differentiable tensor operations:

- **Truth values**: Real numbers in [0, 1]
- **Logical AND**: $x \land y = xy$
- **Logical OR**: $x \lor y = x + y - xy$
- **Logical NOT**: $\neg x = 1 - x$

These operations are differentiable, allowing gradient-based learning of logical rules.

## Neural Architecture for Pattern Matching

Consider a pattern matching task: given a sequence, identify if it matches a regular expression.

Traditional approaches:
1. **DFA compilation**: Fast but inflexible
2. **Neural sequence models**: Flexible but slow

Tensor logic offers a middle ground:

## Applications

1. **Learning regex from examples**: Instead of hand-writing patterns, learn them from data
2. **Fuzzy pattern matching**: Handle noisy inputs gracefully
3. **Compositional generalization**: Combine learned patterns to handle novel situations

## Limitations

- **Computational cost**: Tensor operations are more expensive than discrete DFA transitions
- **Interpretability**: Learned patterns are harder to debug than hand-written regex
- **Theoretical guarantees**: Unlike DFAs, learned matchers don't have formal correctness proofs

## Future Directions

The most promising direction is neuro-symbolic integration: use neural networks for perception and pattern learning, but compile to symbolic representations for verification and deployment.

This mirrors the brain's dual-process theory: fast, intuitive pattern matching (System 1) and slow, deliberate reasoning (System 2).
